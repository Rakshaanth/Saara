Ayurveda
without the
romance tax.

SAARA is the answer to a specific frustration. Walk into Sephora, find an "Ayurveda-inspired" serum, flip the bottle — 0.1% turmeric, 0.2% neem, the rest is filler and a sandalwood story. We grew up watching our mothers and grandmothers use these botanicals at real concentrations, ground fresh, applied generously. This is that. Made clinical. Made for now.

N° 01 / Why we exist
01

The shelf is lying to you.

The Ayurveda section of the modern skincare aisle is a marketing exercise. Brands borrow the words — haldi, neem, kumkumadi, ubtan — sprinkle the actives at homeopathic levels, and price the result like clinical dermatology. Then the marketing team writes a story about a 5,000-year-old tradition.

We did the chemistry. A typical "turmeric brightening serum" on shelves today contains between 0.05% and 0.3% turmeric extract. The published efficacy threshold for tyrosinase inhibition in topical curcuminoids is 0.5% minimum, 1% optimal. The shelf is selling you a flavor, not a function.

SAARA exists because there is no version of this brand we'd want to buy. So we made it. With every concentration on the label, every district on the receipt, every claim tied to a measurable mechanism.

N° 02 / The method
02

Tradition is just long-form data.

For two thousand years before clinical dermatology existed, South Asian households ran an iterative skin-care RCT across forty generations. The compounds that survived — turmeric, neem, kokum, vetiver, centella, rose — weren't selected for their narrative. They were selected because they worked, repeatedly, on millions of skin types, in a tropical climate, with zero placebo control.

That's not folklore. That's the largest uncontrolled observational study in human history, and modern dermatology has spent the last forty years confirming the mechanisms behind the molecules our grandmothers chose intuitively.

Our job isn't to invent. It's to concentrate. Identify the active compound in each traditional botanical, source the highest-grade extract at therapeutic %, formulate at the pH the molecule actually needs, and put the number on the bottle. That's the method. The rest is decoration.

N° 03 / The source
03

A district, not a country.

Every botanical in SAARA names its district of origin. Not "ethically sourced from India." Not "Himalayan." A district. Kokum from Kerala's coastal belt. Bakuchiol from Rajasthan's Aravalli foothills. Vetiver from Bharatpur. Centella from Assam's tea hills. Rose hydrosol from Kannauj's perfume district, distilled the same way since the 1500s.

Specificity is the antidote to greenwashing. A region tells you nothing — it tells the marketing team something. A district tells you whose hands processed the plant, what altitude and rainfall the soil saw, what cooperative was paid, and whether the next harvest is real or aspirational.

We work with eight cooperatives across nine districts. Every shipment is GC-MS verified on arrival for active compound concentration. If a batch under-spec's, it gets rejected and the cooperative gets paid anyway — we eat the cost so they can keep growing.

N° 04 / The founder
04

Built by diaspora. Made for everyone.

SAARA was founded by a 19-year-old Indian-American engineering student running this between MIT research and a transfer year, formulating in collaboration with a cosmetic chemist in Pune and a dermatology consultant in Chicago. The lab work happens in Maharashtra. The clinical validation happens in Boston. The brand operates from Chicago.

The decision to build this came from a specific moment: standing in a Sephora at age seventeen, picking up a $90 "Ayurvedic gold facial oil" with no concentration on the label and a founder photo that wasn't desi. The shelf was selling our grandmothers' formulas back to us, marked up, diluted, and rebranded by people who'd never been in the kitchen when the haldi was ground.

That's not a grievance — it's an opening. The diaspora doesn't owe Ayurveda nostalgia. We owe it precision. SAARA is what happens when the formulation choices get made by someone who grew up watching the methodology, not someone who Googled the vocabulary.

N° 05 / The line
05

What we will not do.

We will never list "ayurvedic blend" or "botanical complex" without the components and percentages broken out. Proprietary blends exist to hide dilution. We're not interested.

We will never claim "ancient wisdom" without naming the specific compound and its mechanism. Romance is not a marketing strategy — it's an insult to the chemistry that actually does the work.

We will never source the same botanical from multiple regions and pretend they're equivalent. Turmeric from Erode is not turmeric from Sangli. Vetiver from Bharatpur is not vetiver from Mysore. The terroir is the product.

We will never expand into categories where we can't formulate at therapeutic concentrations. If we can't beat the clinical reference at the price point, the product doesn't ship. That means three products today. Maybe five next year. Probably not more.

N° 06 / The promise
06

The label is the contract.

Every SAARA bottle ships with a printed Certificate of Analysis taped to the box. Active compound concentration, batch number, GC-MS verification date, cooperative name, harvest date. If a third-party lab tested your bottle tomorrow and the numbers didn't match, you'd have grounds for fraud.

That's the whole brand. The label is the contract. The receipt is the proof. The chemistry is the marketing. Everything else is just essence — saara — distilled down until there's nothing left to hide behind.
